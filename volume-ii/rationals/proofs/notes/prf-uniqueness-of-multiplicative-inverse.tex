\begin{theorem*}[Uniqueness of Multiplicative Inverse]
Let $F$ be a field and let $a\in F$ with $a\neq 0$.  If $b\in F$
satisfies $a\cdot b = 1$, then $b = a^{-1}$.
\end{theorem*}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Professional Standard Proof.}~\\
Since $a\neq 0$ and $F$ is a field, the inverse $a^{-1}$ exists and satisfies
\[
a\cdot a^{-1}=1.
\]
Now compute:
\[
b=b\cdot 1=b\cdot(a\cdot a^{-1})=(b\cdot a)\cdot a^{-1}=(a\cdot b)\cdot a^{-1}=1\cdot a^{-1}=a^{-1},
\]
using associativity, commutativity, the hypothesis $a\cdot b=1$, and the
multiplicative identity axiom.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark*}[Proof structure]
The proof exhibits two right inverses of the same nonzero element $a$:
$b$ from the hypothesis and $a^{-1}$ from the field axiom.  Instead of
invoking cancellation, it inserts the identity $a\cdot a^{-1}=1$ and then
rewrites $b$ by associativity until the hypothesis $a\cdot b=1$ can be
substituted.  The conclusion falls out by a direct chain of equalities.
\end{remark*}
