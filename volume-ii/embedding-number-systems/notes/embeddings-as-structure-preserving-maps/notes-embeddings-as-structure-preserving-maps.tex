\section{Embeddings as Structure-Preserving Maps}
\label{sec:embeddings-as-structure-preserving-maps}

\begin{topicbox}{Embeddings as Structure-Preserving Maps}
\begin{exposition}
The preceding chapters constructed the number systems one at a time. In that
construction-first viewpoint, the symbols used in different systems do not
automatically denote the same object. A natural number, an integer class, a
rational class, and a real number may all be written as \(2\), but the
construction has not made them literally identical.
\end{exposition}

\begin{exposition}
An embedding is the device that repairs this gap in a controlled way. It is
an injective map from one system into another that preserves the relevant
structure: the distinguished constants, the operations, and the order. Once
such a map has been fixed, the smaller system may be identified with its
image inside the larger one. This is why ordinary arithmetic can write
\(\mathbb N\subseteq \mathbb Z\subseteq \mathbb Q\subseteq \mathbb R\), even
though the foundational construction usually passes through maps rather than
literal subset inclusions.
\end{exposition}
\end{topicbox}
